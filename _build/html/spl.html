Stream Power Law

Stream Power Law#

The Stream Power Law (SPL) or Stream Power Incision Model (SPIM) [3] describes the competition between uplift and river incision to control the amplitude of surface topography. Mathematically it is expressed as:

(6)#\[\frac{\partial h}{\partial t}=U-K_fA^mS^n\]

where \(h\) is the elevation of the river bed, \(U\) is tectonic uplift, \(A\) is drainage area, a proxy for water discharge, and \(S\) is slope. \(K_f\) is a rate constant that depends on many parameters including rainfall, lithology or vegetation; \(m\) and \(n\) are exponents. All three are poorly constrained.

Note

A more general version of the SPL assumes that incision by the river is proportional to water discharge, \(\Phi\), which is the integral of the rainfall rate, \(\nu\), over the upstream drainage area, \(A\):

\[\Phi=\int_A\nu\ dA\]

It is commonly assumed that rainfall rate is uniform over a catchment, or, more correctly, that variations in rainfall rate occur on a broader scale than that of a single catchment. In that case, the discharge is equal to the product of the drainage aerea \(A\) by the mean rainfall rate, \(\bar\nu\), which justifies the use of catchment area as a proxy for discharge. It also implies that the rate constant \(K_f\) in equation (6) contains the mean rainfall rate to the power \(m\), i.e., \(K_f\propto\bar\nu^m\).
